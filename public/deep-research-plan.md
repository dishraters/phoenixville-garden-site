# Deep Research Garden Plan — Phoenixville, PA / Zone 7a

## Executive recommendation

The best possible use of the 18 ft × 24 ft full-sun space is **not** to fill every square foot with plants. The highest-success beginner garden is:

1. **Tall fruiting crops across the north/back**: 3 caged tomatoes, 2 peppers, 2 eggplants.
2. **Squash isolated in controlled zones**: 2 zucchini with 3–4 ft circles, 1 spaghetti squash at the far left edge trained outward.
3. **Low herbs and pollinator flowers along the south/front**: parsley, thyme/oregano, marigolds/zinnias.
4. **Clear access paths and mulch**: paths matter as much as crops because harvesting and weekly maintenance prevent the garden from becoming a jungle.
5. **Drip/soaker irrigation + compost + mulch**: the biggest practical upgrade for full-day sun in July/August.

This design wins because it is productive, beautiful, easy to maintain, and deliberately prevents squash from taking over.

## Research basis

Key findings used:

- Penn State’s Philadelphia-area planting guide says warm-season crops like **summer squash, winter squash, cucumbers, melons, and pumpkins** can be planted in early May through mid-June, and **eggplant, peppers, tomatoes, and basil** from mid-May through late June. It also notes central Philadelphia’s average last frost around **April 20**, with outlying areas a week or two later. Phoenixville should be treated as slightly later than central Philadelphia.
- University of Maryland Extension recommends full sun for zucchini/summer squash: at least **6 hours/day**, preferably **8–10 hours/day**. This matches Sam’s “full all-day sunlight” assumption.
- UMD spacing guidance for summer squash: single plants should be **2–3 ft in-row** with **3–5 ft between rows**; hills need even more room. This supports limiting the garden to **two zucchini plants**.
- UMD vegetable planting guidance warns that squeezing plants too close together reduces yield and increases plant problems. This is the reason the design protects pathways and avoids overplanting.
- UMD notes that tomatoes, peppers, eggplants, and herbs establish better from transplants. For this garden, starts/transplants are the right beginner choice.
- UMD planning guidance specifically recommends going vertical to save space because tomatoes need support and winter squash spreads widely.

## Why this is the best layout

### 1. North/back row is for height and structure
Tomatoes are the tallest and need cages/stakes. Keeping them north prevents them from shading herbs and flowers. Peppers and eggplant stay nearby because they also want heat and full sun but are more upright than squash.

### 2. Squash gets space but not permission to invade
Zucchini is productive but huge. Spaghetti squash is worse because it vines. The design gives them dedicated territory and makes the weekly job obvious: redirect vines outward and keep paths clear.

### 3. Front/south row stays beautiful and useful
Parsley, oregano, thyme, marigolds, and zinnias stay low and easy to access. This makes the garden look finished from the front and pulls pollinators into the crop zone.

### 4. Paths protect the harvest
A garden that cannot be entered becomes neglected. The 3 ft middle path and 2 ft front path make pruning, harvesting, watering, and photographing easy.

### 5. Containers solve spread problems
Mint is excellent but aggressive. It belongs in a pot only. The bay tree and hanging baskets should stay outside the bed so they add beauty without stealing vegetable space.

## Exact planting list

### Vegetables
- 1 San Marzano tomato
- 1 grape/cherry tomato
- 1 slicing/sandwich tomato
- 1 poblano pepper
- 1 jalapeño pepper
- 2 eggplants recommended; 3 only if spacing still feels open
- 2 zucchini plants maximum
- 1 spaghetti squash maximum

### Herbs
- 3 basil starts near tomatoes
- 1–3 parsley starts front-middle
- 1 oregano start
- 1 thyme start
- 1 mint start in a container only

### Flowers
- 12–24 marigolds and/or zinnias along the front border, depending on pot size/spacing
- Optional 4 hanging baskets for color

### Containers and infrastructure
- Tomato cages/stakes: 3 heavy-duty cages or stakes
- 1 mint pot, minimum 12 inches wide
- Optional large bay-tree planter
- Soaker hose or drip irrigation
- Compost
- Mulch/straw/leaf mulch
- Plant labels
- Garden twine/clips

## Spacing guide

| Crop | Spacing | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Tomatoes | 24–30 in apart | Cage/stake immediately; prune lower leaves for airflow |
| Basil | 10–12 in apart | Near tomatoes, not jammed against stems |
| Peppers | 18–24 in apart | Full sun, upright, easy to maintain |
| Eggplant | 24–30 in apart | Use 2 plants for easier care; 3 only if roomy |
| Zucchini | 3–4 ft per plant zone | Two plants max; harvest small and often |
| Spaghetti squash | 4–6 ft vine zone | Edge/corner only; train vines outward |
| Parsley | 8–12 in apart | Front-middle for easy picking |
| Oregano/thyme | 12–18 in apart | Low border; trim to keep tidy |
| Marigolds/zinnias | 8–18 in apart | Depends on variety; use as pollinator border |

## Planting timing for Phoenixville

Treat Phoenixville as slightly later than central Philadelphia.

- **Early May**: plant or direct-sow squash only if nights are warm and frost risk has passed.
- **Mid-May to early June**: safest window for tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, basil, zucchini, and spaghetti squash transplants.
- **June–July**: mulch, install/adjust supports, prune tomatoes, keep squash redirected.
- **July–September**: peak harvest; water deeply during heat.
- **Late August–September**: optional fall greens in any open pockets, if desired.

---

## Weekly maintenance checklist

Every week:

- Check soil moisture under mulch.
- Water deeply if dry, preferably at the base of plants.
- Tie tomatoes to cages/stakes.
- Prune tomato leaves touching soil.
- Redirect spaghetti squash vines outward.
- Harvest zucchini small before they become baseball bats.
- Pick herbs to encourage new growth.
- Deadhead flowers if needed.
- Photograph the garden from the same angle for progress tracking.

---

## The “best garden” version in one sentence

A full-sun Phoenixville garden should be **tomatoes/peppers/eggplant structured across the north, squash contained in the middle/edge, herbs and flowers low across the south, with wide paths, mulch, drip watering, and weekly vine control.**
