Phoenixville Garden
Weekly Tracker

18 ft deep × 24 ft wide · Zone 7a · full sun

The best practical garden layout.

Tall crops in the north, squash contained in the middle/edge, herbs and flowers in the south. Simple enough to follow on a phone while planting.

Quick rules

  • North/back = tomatoes, peppers, eggplant
  • Middle/edge = zucchini + spaghetti squash
  • South/front = herbs + pollinator flowers
  • Mint stays in a pot only

Shopping list

Vegetables: 3 tomatoes, poblano, jalapeño, 2 eggplants, 2 zucchini, 1 spaghetti squash.

Herbs/flowers: basil, parsley, oregano, thyme, mint pot, marigolds/zinnias.

Supplies: 3 tomato cages, compost, mulch, soaker hose/drip line, labels, twine.

Tab 2

Why each plant goes there

Short version: keep tall plants north, aggressive plants contained, and frequent-pick plants easy to reach.

Tomatoes + basil → north/back

Tomatoes are tall and need cages. North placement prevents shade over the rest of the garden. Basil likes similar warmth and is easy to tuck nearby.

Peppers → back row beside tomatoes

Poblano and jalapeño want full sun and stay upright. They fit neatly beside tomatoes without spreading.

Eggplant → back/right

Eggplant wants heat, sun, and airflow. Two plants are ideal; three only if spacing still feels open.

Spaghetti squash → far left edge

It vines aggressively. Edge placement lets vines trail outward instead of running through tomatoes and herbs.

Zucchini → middle with room

Zucchini gets big fast. Two plants are plenty. Give each a 3–4 ft zone and keep the path clear.

Parsley, oregano, thyme → front

Low herbs should be reachable from the front and should not shade vegetables.

Marigolds/zinnias → front border

They make the garden look finished and bring pollinators toward the vegetable flowers.

Mint → container only

Mint spreads aggressively. A pot gives the benefit without letting it take over the bed.
